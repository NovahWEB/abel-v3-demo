About Abel

The one who names the invisible.

Field herpetologist, researcher and communicator. This is his story, in his own words.

I grew up in Chiriquí, in the highlands of western Panama, where the forest begins almost in your backyard. As a kid I spent my days chasing whatever moved among the leaves. That curiosity never left; in time it became my work.

I studied Biology at UNACHI, earned a master’s at the Universidad de los Andes in Bogotá, and a doctorate in Natural Sciences at Goethe University Frankfurt, in association with the Senckenberg Institute. But the real training happened in the field, at night, with the headlamp pointed at a frog no one had described yet.

Over more than twenty years of fieldwork I have described or co-described around 35 amphibian and reptile species new to science, and two new genera, nearly all from Panama. On some I am first author; on many, part of a team. I combine classical taxonomy with genetics, bioacoustics and biogeography, because to understand a species is also to understand where it lives and how it changes.

Science does not end in the lab. I am president of Fundación Los Naturalistas, I take herpetology to classrooms and communities, and through Konsenda I share the field with anyone who wants to see it up close. If I learned one thing, it is this: you cannot protect what you do not know, and there is still so much we do not know.

“We don’t know our biodiversity in depth, and we are losing it.”
